From Jenkinsfiles to ArgoCD: a migration field guide

Three years ago I inherited 40-odd Jenkinsfiles and a mandate to move everything to GitLab CI. Here's what the org chart didn't warn me about.

Published March 14, 2026 14 min read By Shanmukha

The migration took nineteen months. Nobody was fired, nothing caught fire, and we shipped a major product launch two weeks before cutover. By every metric that matters it went well — which is exactly why I want to write down the hard parts before I forget them.

I was hired to lead the CI/CD modernization at an org with 65+ microservices across three business units, each with their own release cadence, their own favourite language, and their own feelings about how builds ought to work. The brief was simple: consolidate onto GitLab CI and ArgoCD. The reality, as always, was about people.

What I thought the hard part would be

Going in, I assumed the challenge was technical. YAML conversion. Terraform state. The byzantine ways that Jenkins plugins break when you breathe on them. I spent the first month building elegant GitLab CI templates and felt very clever.

Then I rolled them out to the first team and nothing happened for three weeks.

The pipeline is just the surface. Underneath it is a social contract between developers, release managers, and security — and you can't migrate the surface without renegotiating the contract.

What the hard part actually was

Three lessons, in the order I learned them:

  1. Trust is more expensive than tooling. Teams had spent years memorizing the exact incantations that made their Jenkinsfiles work. Asking them to re-learn felt like I was taking away competence. I had to reframe the migration as giving them time back, not changing how they worked.
  2. The old system has opinions you didn't know about. One team's "simple build" turned out to rely on a 300-line shared library that three other teams also depended on in mutually incompatible ways. We didn't discover this until week six.
  3. GitOps wants a single source of truth. Enterprises hate that. ArgoCD's model assumes Git is the authoritative state. Getting there required deleting a lot of kubectl apply muscle memory from our SREs.

The template that saved us

After three false starts, we landed on a tiered template model. One .gitlab-ci-base.yml with the stages, scans, and deployment jobs everyone needed. Then language-specific overlays — java-spring.yml, go-service.yml, node-api.yml — that teams could include with a single line: 

# .gitlab-ci.yml in any service repo
include:
  - project: 'platform/ci-templates'
    ref: v2
    file: '/base/gitlab-ci-base.yml'
  - project: 'platform/ci-templates'
    ref: v2
    file: '/lang/java-spring.yml'

variables:
  SERVICE_NAME: billing-api
  OWNER_TEAM: payments

That's the whole pipeline config a new service needs. Seven lines. The first time a junior engineer got a service from zero to production in an afternoon, I knew we'd won.

What I'd do differently

If I had to do it again, I'd spend the first month not writing any code — just sitting with each team, reading their existing Jenkinsfiles, and asking "why?" until someone got annoyed. Every shortcut I took in discovery came back as a three-week detour later.

I'd also version my templates from day one. We introduced ref: v2 in month eleven after a breaking change broke eleven teams simultaneously. That's my permanent lesson: shared infrastructure needs the same release discipline as the products it supports.
